The question

You made a lifetime commitment to your faith, then broke it. Doesn't that prove you're no longer trustworthy about anything else? What's stopping you from leaving your wife and kids tomorrow?

That's a reasonable objection to raise, if a little harsh.

Relationships and commitments are, and totally remain, really important to me. I'm fiercely committed to my wife and my children. I'm an outlier in today's employment landscape, having worked for a total of two companies since 2003.

Of course, I'm fallible. I have lots and lots and lots of evidence for that. It's important to be able to deal with being wrong and move on.

But being wrong about an idea in isolation -- one that just sits in your head, and doesn't impact anyone else -- is one thing. Being wrong about something that impacts others -- or one of your relationships -- is a whole other level. (Imagine making a bad medical choice which led to your child's death, for example -- yikes.)

There are a few reasons I can move on and forgive myself from having broken my commitment.

  1. Knowing more about the biochemistry that was going on in my brain at the time, and the strength of the way I was raised, "Me at age 19" was objectively not the best suited to be making lifetime-impacting decisions. (The fact that the religious group even asks children to make a "lifetime commitment", and frames it that way, is suspect, and is a part of the way it protects itself).
  2. My current understanding is that God does not exist. Of all the reasons to break a contract with someone, "the other party turning out to not exist" is a pretty good one.
  3. My other commitments came in conflict with that commitment, and it was impossible to fulfill them all at the same time.

To expand on the final point, having children was a major catalyst to drawing my prior religious commitment into question. I don't think I could have been a great parent while also trying to force myself to accept the cognitive-dissonance-inducing beliefs I'd previously subscribed to.

This is a pretty familiar problem to anyone that grows up and gets married; sometimes your "top level relationship" (your marriage) can put some strains on your "second level relationships" (parents, siblings, etc.)

I have every reason in the world to want to preserve, grow, adapt, and strengthen my relationships with my wife and children, (and all of our family!) and I'll fight for it. Especially since I now think it's probable that this life is the only one I'll have, having it be a good one for me and the people who depend on me is vital.
