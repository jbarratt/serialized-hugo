Survival of the fittest is an obviously flawed idea. If that was true, I'd be running around killing everyone so I could survive!

This is a good example of a straw man argument; "disproving" a theory by applying one's own definition of what that theory means.

This idea seems to be a definition problem. There are several basic meanings of 'fit', and it sounds like we're talking 'Schwarzenegger fitness' here -- the biggest, strongest, bulkiest, healthiest will survive.

The definition of 'fit' that was actually intended by Darwin was (according to Origin of The Species): "better adapted for immediate, local environment." So, we might rephrase the intention it as "survival of the best adapted for the immediate, local environment." This kind of meaning is common in Statistics and Mathematics. "What function is the best fit for the data."

So, there are scenarios where the Darwin-fittest would be the Schwarzenegger-fittest -- perhaps the biggest, baddest male Silverback Gorilla gets to lead the troop, and brute force does rule the day.

But, there are many other (maybe even most?) areas where Darwin-fitness has nothing to do with strength. A frog whose jump reflex is little bit faster can get out of the way of more trucks. A fish which is a little bit smaller can squeeze into more secluded areas in the coral to feed.

Quite often, 'fittest' seems to mean 'most cooperative.' Ants are among the most successful species around, if we consider number and mass of them to be a good measure of success. This is almost entirely due to the way they work as a community. A single ant is an insignificant thing.

Wait, are you saying sometimes evolution would make a species get smaller or simpler? The theory of evolution states all species get more complex and larger over time!

This idea has come up in two separate conversations, and I'm not sure where it comes from. I don't think it's explicitly stated in Darwin's Origin, at the very least.

But no, Evolution does not require species to get more complex or larger over time. It only states that 'fitness', as defined above, will be selected for.

So if we assume life's going along just fine and then BOOM, a massive volcano goes off, clouding the sun for years, lowering the temperature and making food scarce -- then large, complex animals could die off, perhaps going extinct, while smaller/simpler/more fuel efficient animals would at least survive, possibly even thrive.

The general trend we can see played out over the epochs is for more complex mechanisms to be adapted, but there's no law that says things only work that way. In fact, during times of mass extinction (like what seems to have happened with the dinosaurs), a lot of the most complex species died off, causing a temporary reversal of the trend.
